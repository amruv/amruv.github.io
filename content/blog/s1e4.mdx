Now that we've looked into the engine of the transformer - the **Multi-Head Attention** mechanism, let's delve deep into the often less talked about components of the transformer - **Layer Normalisation**, **Residual Connections**, and **(Position-wise) Feed Forward Networks (FFN)**. These are equally important to pay heed to because while the attention mechanism (in all its forms and glory) gathers information, these components understand and stabilise the information and ensure the transformer provides a relevant, context-rich output.

# The Stabilisers - Add & Norm

In deep architectures like transformers, training comes with its own challenges.

1. **Vanishing Gradients** - Learning in deep neural networks happens via backpropagation, sending signals (called learning signals) backwards through the layers to update weights or learn. Every time this signal passes through a complex layer (involving matrix multiplications and activations), it gets attenuated (multiplied by small numbers). In a 100-layer network, by the time the signal reaches the first layer, it's essentially zero. The model stops learning.

2. **Numeric Instability and Exploding Gradients** - Unstable, massive values bouncing around a neural network lead to exploding gradients (learning signal goes to infinity) and numeric instability.

This is why every "thinking" component (MHAs and Feed-Forward Networks) is accompanied by a component that stabilises the "thinking" output by taking care of the above mentioned challenges.

## Add - Residual Connections

A **Residual Connection** allows the bypassing of a component's (or layer's) computation by taking the original input of the component and adding it directly to the output of the component.

If *x* is the input to the component and then *Component(x)* is the calculated output, then:

```
Output = x + Component(x)
```

By adding the original input to the output of a component, this ensures that the learning gradient can never die, or go to 0 and therefore, solves the problem of the vanishing gradient.

## Norm - Layer Normalisation

Normalisation ensures that the inputs to the next layer are on a consistent scale, making the optimisation landscape much smoother and training drastically faster.

After the 'Add' function, the resulting calculation can have significantly varying values (from infinity to -infinity). Normalisation forces these numbers to adhere to a predictable baseline distribution - typically the normal distribution (mean of 0 and variance of 1). This handles the second problem outlined above by pulling all the numbers back to a predictable output.

The final output after the 'Add & Norm' looks something like this:

```
Output = LayerNorm(x + Component(x))
```

# Position-wise Feed-Forward Networks (FFN)

To understand the inner workings of this component, let's break down the name:

- **"Feed-Forward"** - means a simple, vanilla multi-layer perceptron (MLP), where there are no loops or recursion (unlike RNNs) and the data only moves from input to output - strictly forward.

- **"Position-Wise"** - means the exact same neural network is applied to each and every single token (position) independently and in parallel.

FFN consists of two linear transformations that sandwich a non-linear transformation known as an activation function. This structure proposed by [1] of a FFN follows three crucial steps:

**Step 1 - Expansion (W<sub>1</sub>):** Taking in an input vector of dimension, *d<sub>model</sub>* = 512, it is expanded 4x to a dimension, *d<sub>ff</sub>* = 2048. Why expansion? Projecting the input into a higher dimensional space allows the non-linear activation function explained below to disentangle complex, overlapping features within the token's representation.

**Step 2 - Non-Linear Activation (ReLU):** An activation function, ReLU proposed by [1] is applied on to the expanded dimension output of W<sub>1</sub>.

**Step 3 - Contraction (W<sub>2</sub>):** The second transformation shrinks down, or contracts the dimension, *d<sub>ff</sub>* back to dimension, *d<sub>model</sub>* (2048 → 512). This is done to maintain uniformity across the entire transformer block.

Mathematically,

```
FFN(x) = Activation(xW_1 + b_1)W_2 + b_2
```

## Why do we need an FFN?

We've lauded the concept of attention quite a bit during the course of this series, so then why are we still relying on a "neural network"?

Self-attention is essentially a series of linear calculations, and according to math laws, a stack of linear operations (dot product, weighted sums, etc.) leads to just a bigger linear operation. Complex, real-world relationships (between words or tokens) are hard to understand with establishing just linear relationships. The activation layer in the FFN introduces non-linearity and is therefore, essential to learn sets of unpredictable, complex relationships.
